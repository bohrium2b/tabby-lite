Adjudicator Guide

A concise guide for adjudicating at the Macleans College In-School Debating Tournament.

Welcome to the Macleans College In-School Debating Tournament! Adjudicating is one of the best ways to improve your own debating—it forces you to step out of the "heat of the moment" and look at the round through the eyes of an impartial observer.

This guide is designed to help you navigate your role, whether you are judging alone, on a panel, or as a "third team" adjudicator.

TL;DR: The Essentials

  • The Model Judge: You are an "Average Reasonable Person"—you know general current events but have no specialized knowledge. Ignore your personal opinions when deciding the result.
  • The Goal: Determine which team was more persuasive through reasoned argument.
  • Scoring: Use the 60–80 point scale for main speeches and 30–40 for replies. An average speech is 70.
  • The Process: Take notes, identify the main "clashes" (issues), decide who won those issues, and assign scores that reflect that result.
  • The Ballot: Only the Chair submits the official ballot.

1. Your Mindset: The "Average Reasonable Person"

As an adjudicator, you must set aside your personal expertise and biases.

  • Tabula Rasa (Clean Slate): Do not penalise a team for missing an argument you think is important, and do not "fill in the gaps" for a team that has a good idea but fails to explain it.
  • No Specialist Knowledge: If a team gives a fact that you know is slightly wrong, accept it as true unless the other team challenges it, provided it sounds plausible to a regular person.
  • Be Fair: Listen to both sides with equal diligence and track all arguments and Points of Information (POIs).

2. The Three Pillars of Marking

WSDC/ASD style uses a tripartite marking system. Every speech is marked out of 100 (then scaled to 80) based on:

Category Weight What it Covers
Content 40% The "What": arguments, rebuttals, and POIs. Are the logical links strong? Is there evidence?
Style 40% The "How": persuasiveness, eye contact, voice variation, and body language. (Accents are never penalised.)
Strategy 20% The "Why": structure, time management, and prioritising the most important issues.

3. Tournament Specifics: Times & Scores

For this tournament (JV/Development/Open), the format is:

  • Main Speeches: 6 minutes.
  • Protected Time: The 1st and last minute are protected. POIs may be offered between 1:00 and 5:00.
  • Reply Speeches: 3 minutes (given by the 1st or 2nd speaker).

The Scoring Range (Substantive Speeches)

  • 80 (Exceptional): One of the best speeches you've ever heard; flawless logic and delivery.
  • 74–76 (Extremely Good): Very strong, nuanced analysis with minimal gaps.
  • 70 (Average): A solid pass: relevant arguments and clear structure.
  • 64–66 (Competent): Relevant points made but with significant logical gaps.
  • 60 (Minimum): Content is mostly irrelevant or extremely confusing.

Note: Reply speeches are marked out of 50 (30–40 range), where 35 is average.

4. How to Decide the Winner

Do not just add up speaker scores to see who wins. Follow an issues-based approach:

  1. Identify the Clashes: What were the 2–3 main questions the teams were disputing?
  2. Evaluate the Clashes: Who won each issue? Look at the arguments and the rebuttals that followed.
  3. Weigh the Issues: Decide which clash was more important; the team that wins the most important issues usually wins the debate.
  4. The Result: The team that wins the most important issues wins the debate. Your speaker scores must reflect this—do not produce a "low-point win" where the winning team has a lower total speaker score.

5. Judging Roles & Conferral

If you are a Panel or Third Team judge

  • The Conferral: After the debate, the Chair will lead a discussion; everyone should share their initial thoughts on who won and why.
  • Be Open: It is okay to change your mind if a co-judge points out something you missed.
  • Individual Ballots: While you discuss the round together, every judge usually makes their own final decision. In this tournament, only the Chair submits the final ballot.

Delivering the Oral Adjudication (OA)

  1. Announce the Result First: Don't keep teams in suspense.
  2. Be Comparative: Explain why Prop's argument was more persuasive than Opp's rebuttal.
  3. Constructive Feedback: Give specific tips for improvement (e.g., "Speaker 1, try to use more examples in your second point").
  4. Time Limit: Keep the OA under 8 minutes.

6. Quick Pro-Tips

  • POIs: A speaker should ideally take two POIs. Taking zero or only one is a weakness; taking many can ruin the speech flow.
  • New Matter: The 3rd speaker cannot bring entirely new arguments, but may provide new examples and deeper analysis of existing points. Reply speakers cannot bring up new matter.
  • Note-taking: Use a "flow sheet" to track how an argument from 1st Prop is handled through the round.
